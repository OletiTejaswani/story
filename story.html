Story by Madhu Sudhan

Behind Closed Doors

It was a bright morning when little Anya clutched her mother’s hand, her tiny feet hesitant as they approached the grand gates of the hostel. Her parents wore hopeful smiles, confident they were setting her on a path to success. Four-year-old Anya, however, felt a tug of unease. She didn’t understand why she couldn’t stay home, surrounded by familiar warmth, but her parents had said this was “for her own good.”

The hostel looked grand on the outside, a sprawling building with manicured lawns and gleaming windows. But the beauty stopped at the surface. The moment her parents left, Anya felt the change. The warmth was replaced with cold rules, stern voices, and the indifference of those who were meant to care for her.

As days turned into months, Anya learned to survive in an environment that was anything but nurturing. The caretakers, despite their education, seemed to have forgotten what it meant to be human. Punishments were harsh, kindness rare, and the sense of being “just another child” haunted her every moment.

But the worst came when she crossed paths with Raghav, a tenth-grade student. Raghav was charismatic in front of teachers and intimidating in the shadows. He wielded his seniority like a weapon, using it to exploit and manipulate. Anya, young and vulnerable, became his target. His actions weren’t overt at first—a sharp comment here, an inappropriate touch there—but they left Anya confused and scared.

She didn’t know how to express what was happening. The adults, the very people she should have been able to trust, dismissed her fears as childish imagination. “He’s just teasing,” they would say. Anya quickly learned that her cries for help would only echo back at her, unheard.

Her parents, blissfully unaware, visited every few months, bringing sweets and hugs. Anya clung to those moments, wanting to scream the truth but fearing they wouldn’t believe her. How could they? They trusted the hostel, the system, the illusion of discipline and order.

Years later, when Anya was old enough to leave the hostel, the scars remained. She confronted her parents one evening, her voice trembling with suppressed emotion. “Did you know?” she asked. Her mother looked confused. “Know what, Anya?”

“That you sent me to hell.”

It was then her parents realized the price of their trust in the system. They had wanted the best for her but had unwittingly subjected her to years of silent suffering.

This story isn’t just about Anya—it’s about the countless children who grow up in environments where appearances deceive and cries for help are silenced. It’s a reminder that as parents, teachers, and caregivers, it’s not enough to trust systems blindly. Every child deserves to feel seen, heard, and safe, no matter where they are.

Because behind closed doors, even the brightest facades can hide the darkest truths.
